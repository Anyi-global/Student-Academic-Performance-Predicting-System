Insights for School Staff

Simple patterns from past student records that help explain what this tool looks for and how staff can respond early.

Main warning signs Past failures, absences, low study time

These are the signals that show up most often when students start moving off track.

Best use of the score Decide how fast to respond

Use the percentage to judge whether a student needs urgent help, extra support, or normal follow-up.

Main takeaway Act early, then recheck

This tool is most useful when schools step in before attendance or performance problems become harder to reverse.

Early sign

More absences usually mean more concern

Missing school regularly is one of the clearest signs that a student may begin struggling.

Action: Treat rising absences as a reason to check in quickly, not just as an attendance problem.
  • Once absences become frequent, the chance of academic problems rises sharply.
  • Missing lessons often affects homework and class participation before final results drop badly.
  • Simple attendance tracking is one of the fastest ways to spot students who may need support.
Past performance

Previous failures matter a lot

A student who has failed before is more likely to need extra support again.

Action: If a student has failed before, consider tutoring, a clear support plan, and closer follow-up.
  • Even one previous failure can be enough to justify extra attention.
  • Two or more failures usually point to a deeper problem that needs stronger support.
  • Past performance is most useful when reviewed together with current attendance and study habits.
Study routine

Regular study time helps reduce risk

Students with steady weekly study habits are less likely to struggle academically.

Action: If study time is low, help the student build a realistic routine, not just revise content.
  • Students studying very little each week are much more likely to run into problems.
  • A clear routine can matter as much as ability when a student is slipping.
  • Short, consistent study plans are often more helpful than telling a student to simply "work harder."
Main factors

What this tool pays most attention to

The strongest signals are previous failures, absences, and day-to-day habits linked to study and wellbeing.

Action: When several of these warning signs appear together, the student likely needs planned support, not just a reminder.
  • The tool does not react to one answer alone; it looks at how several warning signs combine.
  • Absences and previous failures together are more concerning than either sign on its own.
  • Family background and lifestyle still matter, but direct school warning signs matter more.
Using the score

How to read the risk percentage

The percentage helps you judge how strongly to respond. It should support, not replace, staff judgment.

0% to 39%

Routine monitoring. Keep good routines in place and recheck if attendance, study habits, or engagement begin to slip.

40% to 69%

Targeted support. Review weak subjects, attendance, and missed work before the student moves into a more serious situation.

70% and above

Urgent support. Move quickly with tutoring, family contact, and close follow-up over the next few weeks.

Action: Use the percentage to decide how quickly and how strongly the school should respond.
In practice

Best way to use this in school

These insights are most useful when they lead to action, not just discussion.

  • Use repeated absences and earlier failures as clear reasons for a staff review.
  • Read the score alongside what teachers already see, such as missed assignments, participation, and classroom behavior.
  • Agree on one or two support steps, then check later whether the student is improving.
  • A lower-risk result does not mean "ignore"; it means keep protecting what is working well.
Action: This tool works best when schools combine the score with conversation, support, and follow-up.
